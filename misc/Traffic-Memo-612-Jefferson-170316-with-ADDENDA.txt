This memorandum documents estimated trip, parking and site access analysis related to the 
affordable housing development proposed by Habitat for Humanity of San Francisco at 612 
Jefferson Avenue in downtown Redwood City, California. The Project would add 20 for‐sale, 
affordable housing units and 15 parking spaces on a vacant lot. 
 
Project Setting 
The Project is an infill development in downtown Redwood City (see Figure 1). The site is in 
close proximity to goods and services (such as a grocery store, post office, city hall, hospital, 
restaurants, and theatres) and public transportation. The site is within the area covered by the 
Downtown Precise Plan (DTPP) and its corresponding environmental impact report (EIR). 
 
Public Transportation 
Rail Service:  Caltrain operates commuter trains along the peninsula from Gilroy to San 
Francisco, connecting riders in Redwood City to major employment centers on the peninsula, in 
San Francisco, and in San Jose and to BART rail service in San Bruno and Millbrae. Caltrain runs 
every 6 to 20 minutes during weekday commute hours, and hourly at off‐peak times. The 
Redwood City Caltrain Station is located ¼ mile from the site. 
 
BART provides transit connections to San Francisco International Airport, Oakland International 
Airport and to major employment centers in San Francisco and the East Bay (e.g. Oakland, 
Berkeley, and Emeryville). BART runs approximately every 7 to 10 minutes during weekday 
commute hours and 15 to 20 minutes during off‐peak times.  
 
Bus Service:  San Mateo County Transit District (SamTrans) operates two bus routes that pass in 
front of the project – Routes 270 and 276. Thirteen SamTrans routes stop at the Sequoia 
Station Transit Center, which serve local and regional destinations.

Bicycle & Pedestrian Networks 
Pedestrian Network: Streets surrounding the project site have complete, continuous pedestrian 
facilities. There are continuous sidewalks on Jefferson Avenue from the site to destinations 
throughout the downtown. Intersections typically have high visibility, marked crosswalks and 
pedestrian warning signs (if uncontrolled), accessible curb ramps, and pedestrian signals. Most 
sidewalks have trees planted between the sidewalk and street parking, creating a buffer 
between sidewalks and parked or moving vehicles.  
 
Bicycle Network: There are a combination of bicycle facilities within and connecting to 
Downtown. The following bicycle facilities are close to the project and would connect residents 
riding bicycles to local destinations: 
     Veterans Boulevard, Whipple Avenue to Chestnut Street: buffered bike lanes 
     Marshall Avenue, Walnut Street to Broadway: bike lanes 
     Winslow Avenue, Broadway to City border: bike lanes 
     Brewster Avenue, Arguello Street to Main Street: bike lanes 
Jefferson Avenue between El Camino Real and Veterans Boulevard is classified in the General 
Plan as a Class III shared bike route, but has not yet been implemented.  
 
Trip Generation & Distribution 
Trip generation estimates for the project site were developed using the relevant land use rates 
from the Institute of Transportation Engineers (ITE) Trip Generation, 9th Edition using land use 
category “Residential Condominium/Townhouse” (230). Based on an average trip rate of 5.81 
trips per day per dwelling unit, the project would generate an estimated 117 trips per day, with 
9 trips in the peak AM hour and 11 trips in the PM peak hour. Using the Mixed‐Use 
Development (MXD) model adjustments from the Downtown Precise Plan to reflect the mixed‐
use nature of downtown and non‐driving (walking, biking, transit) trips, these numbers would 
be further reduced to 9 AM trips and 8 PM trips. The MXD model used ITE trip generation rates 
for the initial trip estimate, then calculated a trip reduction percentage to estimate the effect of 
mixed‐use development in the downtown area based on various socio‐economic variables. 
Additional trip reductions were applied to account for transit uses within the downtown area. 
This methodology is fully detailed in the DTPP EIR.  
 
These trips were distributed to intersections around the project site according to the travel 
patterns established in the DTPP, supplemented by information about existing patterns and 
surrounding land uses. Based on these patterns, 43% of trips (4 AM trips/3 PM trips) would 
head east on Jefferson Avenue and 57% (5 AM trips / 5 PM trips) would head west. The result is 
that the Project is estimated to add 3 to 5 trips to nearby intersections.  
 
Parking 
Per the DTPP, the Project is required to provide 28 parking spaces (rounded up from 27.5). This 
is based on the following unit types in the Project: 
     5, 1‐Bedroom units (5 x 1 = 5 spaces) 
     10, 2‐Bedroom units (10 x 1.5 = 15 spaces) 
     5, 3‐Bedroom units (5 x 1.5 = 7.5 spaces) 
 
However, the State Density Bonus law (Govt. 65915) states that the City cannot impose a 
parking ratio (inclusive of handicapped and guest parking) that exceeds 0.5 spaces per 
bedroom.  If the maximum allowed ratio of 0.5 spaces per bedroom were applied to the 
Project, twenty parking spaces would be required.  This is based on the following unit types in 
the Project: 
 
     5, 1‐Bedroom units (5 x 0.5 = 2.5 spaces) 
     10, 2‐Bedroom units (10 x 2 x 0.5 = 10 spaces) 
     5, 3‐Bedroom units (5 x 3 x 0.5 = 7.5 spaces) 
 
As proposed, the Project provides fifteen (15) on‐site parking spaces in a ground‐floor level 
garage.  In addition to the State Density Bonus law limiting the maximum amount of required 
parking to 0.5 spaces per bedroom, DTPP Section 2.6.2.C allows development projects to apply 
to reduce the required number of parking spaces for residential uses with a lower parking 
demand as stated in Section 30.2(a)(4) of the Zoning Ordinance.  Factors to consider in 
evaluating an application for this parking reduction include, but are not limited to, the project’s 
design, location, affordability, unbundled parking pricing, and dwelling unit size.   
 
The Applicant has requested approval for a parking reduction of 5 additional parking spaces, as 
permitted in DTPP Section 2.6.2.C. The justification for this request is the Project’s 100% low‐
income level of affordability and its close proximity to public transit (approximately one‐third of 
a mile from Caltrain and Sequoia Station), goods and services.  Furthermore, the Project 
conditions of approval require a Transportation Demand Management (TDM) plan that will be 
reviewed and approved by the City prior to issuance of a certificate of building occupancy.  The 
objective of the TDM plan is to support project residents in reducing their need for a car and 
corresponding parking spaces. Elements of a TDM plan should be tailored to the specific needs 
of Project residents, but could include membership in a carsharing program (Zipcar currently 
has vehicles in Redwood City), monthly transit passes, a fleet of shared bikes, or similar 
measures that support a car‐free lifestyle.  
 
The Project is also required to provide bicycle parking at a ratio of 1 bicycle parking space per 5 
parking spaces under the DTPP. The Project’s provision of 8 bicycle parking spaces meets the 
City’s requirements.  
 
Intersection Operations 
A traffic study and addendum (Hexagon Transportation Consultants, Inc., dated 4/6/16 and 
addendum dated 4/20/16) were completed for the 92‐unit project across the street at 603 
Jefferson Avenue (across the street from the subject property). Both projects are proposed as 
for‐sale condominium projects and as such their respective traffic analyses are based on same 
Institute of Transportation Engineers’ trip generation rate. The trip generation rate estimates 
(daily, morning and evening peak) trips from each project based on the number of residential 
units. During the course of that project’s approval, it was reduced in size by 23 units – an 
amount greater than the proposed 20‐unit Project.  
 
The analysis from the traffic study had the following findings: 
      The 603 Jefferson Avenue project is estimated to generate a net increase of 
        approximately 465 daily vehicle trips, with 22 trips occurring during the AM peak hour 
        and 39 trips occurring during the PM peak hour. 
      The estimated project trip assignments for the study intersections fall within the trip 
        volumes described in the Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan. 
      Compared to the level of service analysis and trip assignments previously prepared for 
        the Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan, the 603 Jefferson Avenue project would not 
        cause any new impacts or worsen the intersection impacts previously identified. 
 
For the following reasons, the findings of 603 Jefferson Avenue project analysis apply to this 
Project as well: 
      Both projects have the same land use (for‐sale, residential condominiums),  
      Project sites are across the street from one another and due to their proximity and land 
        use, would have similar trip making patterns, and 
     The subject Project, in combination with the approved 68‐unit project at 603 Jefferson, 
      is smaller than the analyzed 92‐unit 603 Jefferson Avenue project.  
 
To mitigate the cumulative impact of project trips on the City’s transportation system, the 
project will pay the City’s Transportation Impact Fee.  
 
Project Impacts 
Due to the location of the project within a developed, downtown location, existing bicycle and 
pedestrian facilities and regular transit service, and inclusion of bicycle parking and a TDM plan, 
the small number of new trips from the project are not expected to adversely impact transit 
service, pedestrian and bicycle facilities, or intersection operations. Impacts to traffic, public 
transportation and parking fit within those impacts identified and analyzed in the DTPP EIR and 
are considered less than significant.

Hexagon Transportation Consultants, Inc. has completed a traffic study for the proposed mixed-use
development at 603 Jefferson Avenue in Redwood City, California. The project site is located at the
southeast corner of Jefferson Avenue and Bradford Street, and is approximately 0.5 acres. The
project area falls within the plan area of the Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan (DTPP).
Currently the existing site includes a single-story commercial building. The proposed project would
demolish the existing building and construct an 8-story apartment building with ground floor
commercial space. The project would include 92 apartments above approximately 4,500 square
feet of commercial space. The proposed project includes parking on the ground floor and
underground garage. The project site and surrounding area are shown on Figure 1.

Outlined below are the project trip generation, project trip distribution and assignment, impact
evaluation of project traffic, review of the proposed site plan, and connectivity to transit facilities.
This traffic study analysis is based on the plans titled “Bradford and Jefferson” provided by BDE
Architecture on October 23, 2015.

Project Trip Generation
The Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan calculated trip generation using the Mixed-Use
Development (MXD) model developed by Fehr & Peers, EPA, and ITE. The MXD model utilized the
ITE trip generation rates for the initial estimate, then calculated a trip reduction percentage to
estimate the effect of mixed-use development in the downtown area based on various socio-
economic variables. This reduction included bicycle and walking trips within the downtown area.
Additional trip reductions from standard ITE rates were applied to account for transit uses within the
downtown area. The complete process is outlined in the Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan
Draft EIR, August 27, 2010.
Since this project is located within the Redwood City downtown area, trip generation for this project
assumed the same process as described above. However, Hexagon utilized the latest edition of the
ITE Trip Generation Manual to conduct the analysis for this study, as opposed to the Redwood City
DTPP which used an older version. Hexagon compared the trips generated by the existing single-
story commercial building currently on site to the trips that would be generated by the proposed
residential and commercial mixed-use development. Trips generated by the existing office and
health club on site were analyzed as a single use General Office Building (ITE Code 710), as
opposed to estimating the trips generated by the two different uses. This approach probably results
in a reduced trip generation estimate for the existing uses, which in turn results in a larger net
increase of trips generated by the proposed mixed-use development. Therefore, the overall net trip
generation estimates are more conservative.
As shown in the Table 1, the project is estimated to generate a net increase of approximately 465
daily vehicle trips, with 22 trips occurring during the AM peak hour and 39 trips occurring during the
PM peak hour.

Review of Proposed Site Plan
This section includes a review of the proposed site plan in accordance with generally accepted
traffic engineering standards to identify any access or circulation issues that should be improved.
This review is based on plans titled “Bradford and Jefferson” provided by BDE Architecture on
March 4, 2016. The site plan is shown on Figure 3.
Site Access and On-Site Circulation
The project proposes driveway entrances/exits on Bradford Street and Jefferson Avenue. The
driveway on Bradford Street would provide access to the ground floor parking garage. The driveway
on Jefferson Avenue would provide access to the secured underground parking.
The ground floor parking garage is proposed to have handicapped and commercial stalls. The
driveway would be approximately 100 feet from the southeast corner of the Jefferson Avenue and
Bradford Street intersection. Currently, there are metered angled parking stalls on the south side of
Bradford Street. Some of these stalls would need to be removed to allow access to the Bradford
driveway and to avoid sight-distance issues for vehicles using the Bradford Street driveway.
The secured parking would be 3 levels of below grade parking for residents. The Jefferson Avenue
driveway would be approximately 100 feet from the southeast corner of the Jefferson Avenue and
Bradford Street intersection. Currently, there are parallel parking stalls on the east side of Jefferson
Avenue. When occupied, some of these stalls could cause sight distance issues for vehicles using
the Jefferson Avenue driveway.
Vehicles that use either the ground floor parking garage or the underground parking garage would
only have one way to enter and exit. There is no on-site vehicular connection proposed between the
two parking areas. The parking aisles typically measure 24’-0”, which would be adequate space to
allow vehicles to turn in and out of parking stalls. The proposed design shows both the ground floor
parking garage and underground garage to have dead-end aisles. Dead-end aisles are acceptable
if the aisles are very short and/or the parking spaces are assigned to particular tenants. Also, the
garage plan shows the end stalls to be right against a wall. It is recommended that the parking
garage include a space at the end of the aisles to allow maneuvering in/out of the end spaces.
Hexagon notes that the garage includes some tandem parking, which should be assigned to
particular tenants.
Pedestrians will have access to the residential and commercial uses from several different
locations. Sidewalks are located along Jefferson Street and Bradford Street. The project will
enhance pedestrian access to the site by adding bulb-outs and high visibility crosswalks to all legs
of the intersection of Jefferson Avenue and Bradford Street. The project proposes doors into the
commercial area from Jefferson Avenue and from the ground level garage parking. The residential
lobby would be accessible from Bradford Street and would provide access to a lounge room, mail
room, and 2 elevators that go to all levels. Pedestrian stairwells to the secured garage and upper
levels of residential units are proposed at the northeast corner and the southwest corner of the
building. These stairwells would be accessible from the sidewalks on Jefferson Avenue and
Bradford Street.

Pedestrian and Bicycle Connectivity to Transit Facilities
Redwood City has requested a review of pedestrian and bicycle facilities between the project site
and the Redwood City Transit Center and Caltrain Station to determine if improvements are
warranted.
The Redwood City Transit Center and Redwood City Caltrain Station are both located southeast of
the intersection of California Street and Broadway, approximately 2,000 feet from the project site.
The Redwood City Transit Center and Redwood City Caltrain have pedestrian and bicycle
entrances at the Broadway Street/Marshall Street and Arguello Street intersection and the Winslow
Street and Hamilton Street intersection. Caltrain provides commuter rail service between San
Francisco and Gilroy. The transit center serves as a stop for SamTrans routes 270, 273, 274, 275,
276, 296, 297, 397, 398, and ECR.
Pedestrian Connectivity to Transit Facilities
Pedestrian access from the site to the transit center is good. Pedestrians accessing the transit
center from the project site would walk south along Jefferson Avenue and west on Marshall Street.
Sidewalks are provided on both sides of Jefferson Avenue and Marshall Street. Crosswalks and
pedestrian signal heads are provided at all the intersections. Pedestrian connectivity between the
project site and the transit center is continuous along this route.
Alternatively, pedestrians accessing the transit center from the project site would walk south along
Jefferson Avenue, west along Broadway, and then south on Hamilton Street. Sidewalks are
provided along all the roadway segments and crosswalks are provided at all the intersections.
Pedestrian signal heads are provided at the signalized intersections of Jefferson Avenue/Marshall
Street and Jefferson Avenue/Broadway. Pedestrian connectivity between the project site and the
transit center is continuous along this route.
In addition, the project would include new bulb outs and crosswalk striping at each leg of the
Jefferson Avenue and Bradford Street intersection.
Bicycle Connectivity to Transit Facilities
Currently, there are Class II bike lanes on both sides of Marshall Street from Jefferson Avenue to
the Broadway Street/Marshall Street and Arguello Street transit station entrance. There are no
bicycle facilities on Jefferson Avenue, however, the low traffic volume makes the roadway
conducive to bicycle traffic.

Traffic Impacts and Recommendations
The key findings and recommendations based on the traffic study for the proposed-mixed use
development at 603 Jefferson Avenue in Redwood City are listed below.
      The project is estimated to generate a net increase of approximately 465 daily vehicle trips,
       with 22 trips occurring during the AM peak hour and 39 trips occurring during the PM peak
       hour.
      The estimated project trip assignments for the study intersections fall within the trip volumes
       described in the Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan.
      Compared to the level of service analysis and trip assignments previously prepared for the
       Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan, the project would not cause any new impacts or
       worsen the intersection impacts already identified.
      Some metered parking stalls on the south side of Bradford Street will need to be removed to
       allow access to the proposed Bradford Street driveway.
      Some metered parking stall on the east side of Jefferson Avenue may need to be removed
       to avoid any sight distance issues for vehicles using the Jefferson Avenue driveway.
      The project will enhance pedestrian access by building bulb-outs and high visibility
       crosswalks at the intersection of Jefferson Avenue and Bradford Street.
      The parking garage should include space at the end of the dead-end aisles to allow
       maneuvering in/out of the end spaces.
      All tandem stalls should be assigned to particular tenants.

Redwood City Downtown Precise Plan (DTPP). The purpose of this analysis is to establish whether
the transportation impact conclusions from the DTPP are still valid and to determine if changes in
traffic conditions within the downtown would likely result in a new significant impact.

Project Information
The project site is located at the southeast corner of Jefferson Avenue and Bradford Street, and is
approximately 0.5 acres. The project area falls within the plan area of the Redwood City Downtown
Precise Plan (DTPP). Currently the existing site includes a single-story commercial building. The
proposed project would demolish the existing building and construct an 8-story apartment building
with ground floor commercial space. The project would include 92 apartments above approximately
4,500 square feet of commercial space.
